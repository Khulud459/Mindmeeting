What is a Mind Meeting?

A format built for thinking together

The traditional conference model — speaker on stage, audience in seats — is being challenged. Mind Meetings flip that dynamic entirely.

A Mind Meeting is a structured, participant-driven session where attendees collectively define the agenda, share expertise, and co-create knowledge through guided dialogue and collaborative problem-solving.

Step by Step

How a Mind Meeting works

Click each step to discover what happens — and why it matters for engagement.

01
Set the Stage

The facilitator introduces the theme and ground rules, creating a psychologically safe space where all voices are equal.

🎯 Why it matters: Safety reduces social anxiety and lowers the barrier to participation. When people feel seen and respected, they engage more authentically.

Click to expand

02
Capture the Questions

Every participant writes their burning questions or challenges on cards — no screening, no hierarchy. The room's real needs emerge.

💡 Why it matters: This shifts agenda-setting power from organisers to attendees. The session becomes immediately relevant to everyone in the room.

Click to expand

03
Vote and Cluster

Participants vote on which topics to explore. Similar questions are clustered together, revealing the group's collective priorities.

🗳️ Why it matters: Democratic prioritisation creates shared ownership. Attendees invest more deeply in conversations they helped choose.

Click to expand

04
Rotate and Explore

Small groups form around each top topic. Groups rotate so every participant engages with multiple conversations and diverse perspectives.

🔄 Why it matters: Rotation ensures breadth of exposure and prevents echo chambers. Each group builds on what the previous one started — knowledge compounds.

Click to expand

05
Harvest Insights

A note-taker captures key ideas from each table. Insights are gathered, synthesised, and shared back with the whole group.

📝 Why it matters: Making thinking visible validates contributions and creates a tangible output. Attendees leave with something real — not just a memory of a conversation.

Click to expand

06
Reflect and Close

The facilitator guides a whole-group reflection. Participants articulate one thing they're taking away and one action they'll take.

✨ Why it matters: Structured reflection deepens learning. Committing to an action publicly increases follow-through and makes the event professionally meaningful.

The Science Behind It

Why Mind Meetings work

Four powerful theories explain why active, participatory formats produce deeper engagement and better learning than passive ones. Here's each one — in plain language.

Learning Theory
Kolb's Experiential Learning
David Kolb, 1984

Kolb argued we learn best through a four-stage cycle: having an experience, reflecting on it, drawing lessons, and trying something new. Simply hearing information isn't enough — we need to do and feel.

In a Mind Meeting… Participants experience real dialogue, reflect through structured close, abstract by connecting ideas to their work, and experiment by committing to an action. The full cycle completes in one session.
Social Learning
Vygotsky's Social Learning
Lev Vygotsky, 1978

Vygotsky believed learning happens between people, not just within them. The "Zone of Proximal Development" — the gap between what you can do alone and what you can do with support — is where the richest growth occurs.

In a Mind Meeting… Peers act as co-learners and mentors simultaneously. Rotating groups ensure everyone encounters ideas slightly beyond their current thinking, expanding their ZPD through social scaffolding.
Optimal Experience
Flow Theory
Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, 1990

Flow is the state of complete absorption in a task — when challenge and skill are perfectly balanced. In this state, time disappears, motivation is intrinsic, and performance peaks.

In a Mind Meeting… Participant-chosen topics ensure relevance. Facilitated small groups balance challenge with support. The result? Conversations that feel effortless yet deeply productive — the hallmarks of flow.
Motivation
Self-Determination Theory
Ryan & Deci, 2000

SDT identifies three core human needs that drive intrinsic motivation: autonomy (choosing your path), competence (feeling capable), and relatedness (connecting with others). When all three are met, engagement is deep and lasting.

In a Mind Meeting… Autonomy: participants shape the agenda. Competence: everyone's expertise is valued. Relatedness: collaborative structure builds real connections. All three needs are addressed simultaneously.

Summary

Key takeaways

Everything you need to remember — distilled into seven essential ideas.

1

Mind Meetings put participants in charge. The agenda is co-created by the people in the room, making sessions instantly relevant and personally meaningful.

2

Structure enables freedom. Facilitation provides the container; participants fill it with authentic, valuable content. Both elements are essential.

3

Learning requires doing. Kolb's cycle reminds us that passive listening produces shallow retention. Action, dialogue, and reflection produce lasting change.

4

Other people are the curriculum. Vygotsky showed us that peers expand what we're capable of. Diverse small groups are powerful learning environments.

5

Challenge + relevance = flow. When topics are chosen by participants and conversations are facilitated well, engagement becomes effortless and deep.

6

Autonomy, competence, and connection drive motivation. SDT explains why Mind Meetings feel different: they address all three core human needs simultaneously.

7

The format is transferable. You can apply these principles to workshops, team meetings, community events, and professional conferences at any scale.
